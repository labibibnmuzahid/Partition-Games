CRPS: Combinatorial Row-Partizan Splits

CRPS is a partizan variant of CRIS (Combinatorial Row/Column Independent Splits) where players have asymmetric move sets based on their role, creating strategic depth through player-specific constraints and independent fragment management.

Game Setup

The game begins with a Young diagram (partition) represented as a grid of squares. This initial configuration forms the first fragment.

Player Roles & Move Restrictions

Asymmetric Move Sets:

  • Player A (Red): Can ONLY remove entire rows from any fragment
  • Player B (Blue): Can ONLY remove entire columns from any fragment

This creates a partizan game where players have fundamentally different capabilities, unlike the symmetric nature of the original CRIS game.

Fragment Independence

Key Concept: Independent Fragments

When a move splits a rectangular region into disconnected pieces, each piece becomes an independent fragment. Players can then choose to operate on any fragment on their turn.

Fragment Splitting Rules:

  • When Player A removes a row, any resulting disconnected pieces become separate fragments
  • When Player B removes a column, any resulting disconnected pieces become separate fragments
  • Each fragment can be targeted independently on subsequent moves
  • Fragments retain their positions but operate as separate game components

Strategic Implications

Partizan Strategy Considerations:

  • Asymmetric Threats: Player A controls row-based fragmentation, Player B controls column-based splits
  • Fragment Control: Creating favorable fragment shapes for your move type while limiting opponent opportunities
  • Endgame Planning: Each player must consider different terminal positions based on their move restrictions
  • Piece Coordination: Unlike CRIS, players cannot freely choose move types, requiring different tactical approaches

Advanced Tactics

  • Fragment Generation: Create multiple small fragments to limit opponent options
  • Shape Manipulation: Force fragments into shapes that favor your move type
  • Tempo Control: Use your unique move capabilities to control game pace
  • Zugzwang Positions: Force opponent into positions where any move worsens their situation

Game Theory Properties

CRPS belongs to the class of partizan combinatorial games because:

  • Players have different move sets (asymmetric)
  • Perfect information with no randomness
  • Finite game tree (game must eventually end)
  • Normal play convention (last player to move wins)
Computational Complexity: The analysis of CRPS positions requires partizan game theory rather than the Sprague-Grundy theorem used for impartial games like the original CRIS.

Victory Conditions

A player wins when their opponent cannot make a legal move.

  • Player A wins if no fragments have removable rows when it's Player B's turn
  • Player B wins if no fragments have removable columns when it's Player A's turn
  • The asymmetric nature means different types of positions are winning for each player

Comparison with Related Games

CRPS vs. CRIS:

  • Move Freedom: CRIS allows any player to choose rows or columns; CRPS restricts by player
  • Strategic Depth: CRPS adds positional evaluation complexity due to asymmetric capabilities
  • Game Length: CRPS games may be shorter due to more constrained move options

CRPS vs. CRPM:

  • Fragment Behavior: CRPS maintains independent fragments; CRPM merges remaining pieces
  • Board Evolution: CRPS creates spreading fragment arrays; CRPM maintains single connected partition
  • Complexity: CRPS fragment management adds additional strategic layers
Meta-Strategy: In CRPS, mastering both your own capabilities and understanding your opponent's limitations is crucial for optimal play. The game rewards players who can think asymmetrically about position evaluation.
